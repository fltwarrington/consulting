Case Studies

A pattern runs through every project: a complicated, manual process; a careful read of what's really going wrong; and a practical system that makes the work easier, faster, and more reliable.

Decision-Support · Leadership Program

The selection committee that ran on paper and stick figures

A statewide leadership organization selected its class through a complex, committee-driven process — balancing application scores, references, geography, gender, age, industry, and diversity goals, all at once.

Decision-support designMulti-factor optimizationData modelingExecutive reportingStakeholder workflow
The friction

Committee members tracked demographic balance on large paper charts with stick figures posted around the room. As applicants moved between Accepted, Hold, Alternate, and Declined throughout the day, every board had to be updated by hand — slow, error-prone, and impossible to see clearly in the moment.

The system

A real-time decision-support tool, built in Excel, that tracked applicant status, calculated demographic balance, displayed geographic representation, and supported live scenario analysis — updating the full class composition instantly as decisions changed.

The outcome
  • Eliminated the manual tracking process entirely
  • Gave the committee real-time visibility during selection meetings
  • Supported more informed, more confident decisions
  • Adopted as the standard process — reused year after year
Operations Platform · E-commerce

Positioning the price

Anchor the conversation to the cost of the problem — the staff hours lost to manual work, a stalled or failed migration, decisions made on numbers no one trusts — not to hours spent. Lead with a paid discovery so price follows a shared understanding of scope, present two or three options rather than a single take-it-or-leave-it number, and keep one-time builds clearly separate from ongoing retainers in both the proposal and the invoice.

Sample posts — ready to adapt
Pillar 01 · StoryStory → lesson
A leadership committee once tracked its entire selection process with stick figures taped to the wall. Real paper charts. Hand-drawn figures for gender, geography, age, industry. Every time an applicant moved from "Hold" to "Accepted," someone updated every board by hand. It wasn't that they lacked smart people. The process had simply outgrown its tools. We replaced the wall with a real-time tool that recalculated the whole class the instant a decision changed. Same committee, same criteria — but now they could see the trade-offs live and decide with confidence. The lesson I keep relearning: when a team is buried in manual tracking, they don't need to work harder. They need a system that does the tracking for them. What's your organization still tracking by hand?
Pillar 02 · POVContrarian take
Your new association software won't fix your data. That's not what you're paying them for. A hard truth I've watched play out across many implementations: the vendor configures the platform. Getting your data ready to live in it? That's on you. So go-live arrives and the duplicates come too. The missing fields. The membership types that mean three different things across three departments. Reports no one trusts. The fix isn't a better vendor. It's doing the unglamorous work first: • Inventory every place your data actually lives • Clean, de-duplicate, and standardize before you move • Map your real relationships to the new model • Test with a small batch before the big migration Boring? Completely. It's also the difference between a launch and a year of cleanup. Migrating soon? Start the data conversation before the software conversation.
Pillar 04 · FrameworkListicle
"Should we use AI for this?" Here's the test I actually use. AI is a tool, not a strategy. Before I add it to any workflow, it has to pass three questions: 1. Is there a real, repetitive task underneath it? (If the process is broken, AI just breaks it faster.) 2. Can a human still check the output where it matters? (Trust, but verify.) 3. Does it beat the simpler option? (Sometimes a rule or a template wins.) If it passes, AI can be genuinely transformative — drafting, classifying, summarizing, enriching. If it doesn't, I leave it out and tell the client why. The goal was never "use AI." The goal is a system that works. Where has AI actually earned its place in your operation?
Pillar 03 · ValueQuick tip
If your business runs on ten disconnected tools, the problem usually isn't the tools. It's the handoffs between them. A lead comes in here. The project lives there. Client info is in a spreadsheet. Someone copies and pastes between all of it — and that someone is your bottleneck and your single point of failure. You rarely need to replace the tools. You need: • One source of truth for your core data • Automated handoffs so nothing depends on someone remembering • Documentation so the system isn't trapped in one head That's the difference between software you own and software that owns you. What's the most annoying copy-paste step in your week? I'll suggest how I'd automate it.
